## a/an vs one

| Word | Use | Example |
|---|---|---|
| a/an | name a singular noun; number unimportant | I need **a** ticket. |
| one | the number matters (not two+) | I only need **one** ticket. |
| one | fixed uses / pronoun | **one** day; the red **one** |

## When to use each

### 1. a/an — just naming it

- She's **a** nurse. Can I have **a** glass of water?

### 2. one — the number is the point

- I have **one** brother and two sisters. Just **one** slice, thanks.

### 3. one — fixed phrases and as a pronoun

- **One** day I'll travel the world. He's **one of** my closest friends. I'll take the blue **one**.

> ⚠️ Compare: *I'd like **a** coffee* (naming a drink) vs *I'd like **one** coffee, not two* (stressing the number).

## Common mistakes

**1. 'one' before a job.** *~~She's one teacher~~* → **She's a teacher.**

**2. 'a' when the number matters.** *~~I have a brother, not two~~* (to stress the number) → **I have one brother, not two.**

**3. 'a' as a pronoun.** *~~I'll take the small a~~* → **I'll take the small one. Pro tip:** a/an = just naming it; one = the number matters or replacing a noun.
