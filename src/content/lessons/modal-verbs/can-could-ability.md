## How to use can, could and be able to

| Meaning | Form | Example |
|---|---|---|
| Present ability / possibility | can + base verb | **She can** speak three languages. |
| Past general ability | could + base verb | **I could** run fast as a child. |
| Polite request | Could you…? | **Could you** open the window? |
| Infinitive / perfect forms | be able to | I'd love **to be able to** ski. |

Modals like *can* and *could* never change form (he can, she can) and are always followed by the **base verb** with no *to*.

## When to use each

### 1. Can — present ability and possibility

- **I can swim**, but I **can't dive**.
- You **can pay** by card here. (it's possible / allowed)

### 2. Could — past ability and polite requests

- At five, she **could read** whole books. (general past ability)
- **Could you help** me for a second? (polite request)

### 3. Be able to — the missing forms

- I want **to be able to** cook properly. (after another verb)
- I **haven't been able to** reach him all day. (perfect tense)

> ⚠️ Typical error: for one successful past action, use *was/were able to* or *managed to*: *We were able to book a table* — not ~~we could book a table~~.

## Common mistakes

**1. Adding 'to' after can/could.** *~~I can to swim~~* → **I can swim.**

**2. Adding -s for he/she.** *~~She cans drive~~* → **She can drive.**

**3. Using 'could' for one past success.** *~~I could pass the exam~~* → **I was able to pass the exam. Pro tip:** 'could' = general past ability; 'was able to' / 'managed to' = one specific time it worked.
