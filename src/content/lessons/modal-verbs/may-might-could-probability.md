## How to use may, might and could

| Meaning | Form | Example |
|---|---|---|
| Present / future possibility | may / might / could + base verb | **It might rain** later. |
| Negative possibility | may not / might not + base verb | **She might not come.** |
| Formal permission | may + base verb | **You may leave** the room. |
| Past guess | may / might / could + have + participle | **He may have forgotten.** |

All three take the **base verb** and never change form (it may, she might). *May* is the most formal.

## When to use each

### 1. Saying something is possible

- **It may be** true, but I doubt it.
- **She might be** stuck in traffic.
- **That could be** the answer.

### 2. Negative possibility

- **They might not know** about the change. (perhaps they don't)

### 3. Permission (formal)

- **You may begin** the exam now.

> ⚠️ Typical error: *couldn't* means impossible, not 'possibly not': *He might not be home* (perhaps he isn't) vs *He couldn't be home — I just saw him at work* (it's impossible).

## Common mistakes

**1. Adding 'to' after the modal.** *~~It might to rain~~* → **It might rain.**

**2. Using 'couldn't' for a negative guess.** *~~She couldn't be tired~~* (meaning 'perhaps she isn't') → **She might not be tired.**

**3. Wrong past form.** *~~She might forgot~~* → **She might have forgotten. Pro tip:** may/might/could + base verb = a guess now; may/might/could + have + participle = a guess about the past.
