## between vs among

| Word | Use | Example |
|---|---|---|
| between | two things, or separate/named items | **between** the bank and the café; **between** the three of us |
| among | in a group or mass (as a whole) | a house **among** the trees; popular **among** teenagers |

## When to use each

### 1. between — separate items

- The shop is **between** the bank and the post office.
- We split the bill **between** the four of us. (each person is separate)

### 2. among — a group seen as a whole

- She felt safe **among** friends. The letter was hidden **among** the papers.

### 3. between you and me

- **Between you and me**, I think he's wrong. (object pronoun 'me', not 'I')

> ⚠️ 'Between' isn't only for two — use it when the items are distinct: *a treaty between the five nations.* Use *among* for an undivided group: *support among voters.*

## Common mistakes

**1. 'among' for two separate things.** *~~a fight among the two boys~~* → **a fight between the two boys.**

**2. 'between' + a single group noun.** *~~between the crowd~~* → **among the crowd.**

**3. 'between you and I'.** *~~between you and I~~* → **between you and me. Pro tip:** between = separate items; among = a group or mass.
