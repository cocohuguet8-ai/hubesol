## How to form the future with will

| Form | Structure | Example |
|---|---|---|
| Affirmative | subject + will (**'ll**) + base verb | She **will call** later. |
| Negative | subject + won't + base verb | They **won't come**. |
| Question | Will + subject + base verb? | **Will** you **help**? |

**Will** is the same for every subject — it never adds *-s* or changes. In speech and informal writing we usually contract it to **'ll** (*I'll, she'll*), and the negative is **won't**.

## When to use will

### 1. Instant decisions

A decision made at the moment of speaking.

- The phone's ringing. — Don't worry, **I'll get** it.
- **I'll have** the salad, please.

> ⚠️ Typical error: ~~I'm going to get it.~~ (deciding right now) → For a decision made as you speak, use will: *I'll get it.*

### 2. Predictions and opinions

Often with *I think, probably, maybe*.

- I think it **will rain** tomorrow.
- **You'll love** this film.

> ⚠️ Typical error: ~~It rains tomorrow.~~ → A prediction takes will: *It will rain tomorrow.*

### 3. Promises, offers and requests

- I promise **I'll call** you tonight.
- **Will** you **open** the window?

## Common mistakes

**1. Using will for plans you already made.** If the plan existed before now, use *going to* or the present continuous. *~~I will meet Sara tomorrow~~* (already arranged) → **I'm meeting Sara tomorrow.**

**2. Changing the form of will.** Will never adds *-s*. *~~She wills come~~* → **She will come. Pro tip:** one form fits every subject.

**3. Using the present simple for predictions.** *~~In 2050, cars drive themselves~~* → **In 2050, cars will drive themselves.**
