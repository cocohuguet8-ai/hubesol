## How to form the first conditional

| Clause | Verb form | Example |
|---|---|---|
| If-clause (the condition) | if + present simple | **If you study** hard, |
| Main clause (the result) | will (**'ll**) + base verb | **you'll pass**. |

The if-clause uses the **present simple** — never 'will'. The result clause uses **will** (or a close alternative: be going to, can, might, or an imperative).

## When to use the first conditional

### 1. A real future possibility

- **If I have** time, **I'll call** you.
- **If we leave** now, **we'll catch** the train.

### 2. Warnings, promises and offers

- **If you don't hurry**, **you'll miss** the bus. (warning)
- **If you help** me, **I'll buy** you dinner. (promise)

> ⚠️ Typical error: ~~If it will rain, I'll stay home.~~ → The if-clause takes the present simple: *If it rains, I'll stay home.*

### 3. Other results

You can replace 'will' with a modal or an imperative.

- **If you finish** early, you **can go** home.
- **If you see** Ana, **tell** her to call me.

## Common mistakes

**1. Using 'will' in the if-clause.** *~~If it will rain, we'll cancel~~* → **If it rains, we'll cancel.**

**2. Using the present simple in the result.** *~~If I have time, I call you~~* → **If I have time, I'll call you. Pro tip:** present simple in the if-clause, will in the result.

**3. Using it for imaginary situations.** For something unlikely or unreal, use the second conditional. *~~If I was a bird, I'll fly~~* → **If I were a bird, I'd fly.**
