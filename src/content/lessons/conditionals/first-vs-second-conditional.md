## The difference in one table

| | First conditional | Second conditional |
|---|---|---|
| Meaning | Real, likely future | Imaginary or unlikely |
| If-clause | present simple | past simple |
| Result | will + base verb | would + base verb |
| Example | **If it rains**, **I'll stay** home. | **If I won**, **I'd travel**. |

The first conditional says *this could really happen*. The second says *this is just imagination*.

## When to use each

### First — you think it's likely

- **If I have** time tonight, **I'll call** you. (I probably will have time)

### Second — imaginary or unlikely

- **If I had** a private jet, **I'd fly** everywhere. (I don't have one)
- **If I were** you, **I'd apologise**. (advice, imagining being you)

> ⚠️ Same idea, different likelihood: *If I win the lottery, I'll buy a house* (I bought a ticket) vs *If I won the lottery, I'd buy a house* (just dreaming).

## Common mistakes

**1. Mixing the two.** *~~If I had time, I'll help~~* → choose one: **If I have time, I'll help** (real) or **If I had time, I'd help** (imaginary).

**2. Using 'will' or 'would' in the if-clause.** *~~If it will rain~~ / ~~If I would win~~* → **If it rains / If I won.**

**3. 'Was' instead of 'were' in the second.** *~~If I was you~~* → **If I were you. Pro tip:** ask yourself "could this really happen?" Yes → first; no → second.
